The process

Understanding the problem



First off... who are the users?

PARENTS
Parents who want to register their kids in the SDC programs

VOLUNTEERS
High school students who want to give back through supporting youth with disabilities, and primarily help through supporting and assisting SDC teachers during programs

PROGRAM COORDINATORS/ADMIN
Staff who would be managing the platform, posting classes

TEACHERS
Staff who teach at the programs and work with youth hands-on


Constraints & requirements
  • Multi-language platform (4 languages!)
  • Background check & proof of income document upload
  • Mobile & desktop friendly
  • 1 platform with different access points for different users


Letâ€™s do some (secondary) research!

User interviews were conducted with volunteers and program coordinators/teachers. This led to insightful knowledge and helped squash any assumptions we still had.

After collecting all research and scoping knowledge of users and the problem, we held a discovery session in which we mapped the pain points, opportunities, and goals of each user. This was helpful in ensuring we all had an aligned vision for the product, and reinforcing our knowledge of each users. We also proceeded to map user journeys for each user, to really understand their experience through the processes they encounter.






Problem Statements

PARENTS
A parent needs a way to save their registrant information so that they can easily register their child for a program/class

VOLUNTEERS
A volunteer needs a way to save their registrant information so that they can easily register to volunteer for a program/class

PROGRAM COORDINATORS/ADMIN
A program coordinator needs a way to manage information gathered in the parent and volunteer registration so that they can easily manage programs

TEACHERS
A teacher needs a way to have full awareness of participant information while planning the program before class starts so that they can cater their planning based on their students needs

Let's get testing



User testing

After doing more than 4 different mobile explorations and experimenting with 3 different versions of lo-fidelity designs, we put together a mobile prototype that we sent to parents & volunteers for user testing. We used Maze, a user-testing platform that allowed us to see how users directly interacted with our prototype. We were able to see many statistics and heat maps from the testing, and were able to get questions we had for them asked within the same platform.

Key Insights Action Items
Parents had difficulty using language selector on the main page; not clear enough where to change language since the entire page was english. Worked on developers on clearer options for language selection; decided that language selection would pop up the first time users landed on a page and the choice would be stored in a cookie.
Varying level of ease for parents in signing up for an account; one tester notably kept starting the sign up process then clicking X and going back to the first sign up page and then starting and clicking the back button. Looked like tester was confused about back versus X button, appeared as though they were trying to use a back button on the Sign Up page that wasn't there. Made sure to double check that we had the back button on all pages we needed it on, as well as the X button. Added the text "Back" to make it clearer the function of the back arrow.
Misclicks on classes page, which could have been because they were confused on which class to click in the prototype, but also got us thinking about comparing classes. Decided to switch to popup rather than a page to make it easiser to compare all the details.
Volunteers had a really easy time with the platform! Testing led to positive results.

Making design decisions



Design Decision #1: Reminder & Skip upload option

One key decision made was prompting/reminding the user to submit documents within the flow. A screen giving users information on the required document they must submit, along with prompts to upload documents now or to skip for later. This opportunity/reminder shows up in the following flows, and is justified as follows:

Scenario Reason for Placement in Scenario
When signing up for an account, the screen would appear and ask the user to submit the required document or skip. Placing it in the first flow, sign up, will introduce the need for the document early on. It will make the user aware that this is a document they should need to upload later on.
When registering for a program, whether the screen shows up is dependent on whether they have submitted the document, and the status of the document. It is still important to remind users during this flow to submit documents if they havenâ€™t, again so that they are aware of what is missing and what obligations they will need to fufill if they donâ€™t. Placing it in this flow will hopefully encourage users to upload their documents.



Design Decision #2: Notice on main page

In a similar vein to the decision above, it was also important for us to make users aware that they havenâ€™t submitted certain documents yet. We want to encourage and remind them too. In addition to placing prompts within the sign up and registration flow, a decision we made was to include a notice on the home page when logged into their account, if they have not submitted their document yet.
In terms of interaction, this notice would remain on the page until
a) The user closes the notice, in which this notice would reappear the next time they log in
b) The document has been submitted
